Grammar 1: Two-Part Verbs (Separable Phrasal Verbs)

Particle Position Rules

With a noun object → particle can go before or after the object:

Turn down the TV.  =  Turn the TV down.
Put away your books.  =  Put your books away.

With a pronoun object → particle MUST go after the pronoun:

Turn it down.   ✗ Turn down it.
Put them away.   ✗ Put away them.
핵심 규칙: 명사 목적어 = 순서 자유 / 대명사 목적어 = 반드시 동사와 전치사 사이에! 한국어에는 이 규칙이 없어 혼동하기 쉽습니다.

Responding to Requests with will

Request → Response Pattern

When someone makes a request, we respond with will to show willingness:

A: Please turn down the music.
B: OK, I’ll turn it down.
A: Put away your books, please.
B: All right. I’ll put them away.
A: Can you clean up this mess?
B: Sure. I’ll clean it up right now.
패턴: 요청 시 명사 사용 → 응답 시 대명사로 바꿔서 “I’ll + 동사 + 대명사 + 전치사”로 답합니다.

Common Mistakes: Two-Part Verbs

✗ Please turn off it. / I’ll pick up them.
✓ Please turn it off. / I’ll pick them up.
대명사(it, them)는 동사와 전치사 사이에 넣어야 합니다. 전치사 뒤에 올 수 없습니다.
✗ I cleaned up it already.
✓ I cleaned it up already.
과거시제에서도 같은 규칙 적용: 동사 + 대명사 + 전치사 순서.
✗ Can you turn the light down? (의미: 불 꺼줘)
✓ Can you turn the light off? / Can you turn off the light?
turn down = 줄이다 / turn off = 끄다. 전치사에 따라 의미가 달라지므로 구분하세요!
✗ I’ll throw away the them.
✓ I’ll throw them away.
대명사에 관사(the)를 붙이지 마세요. them 자체가 “그것들을”이라는 뜻입니다.

Grammar 2: Requests with Modals

Modal + Base Form of Verb

Use modals to make polite requests. Formality increases ↓

Can you keep your cat inside? (informal)
Could you turn the music down, please? (polite)
Would you please park your car in your space? (more polite)
공손도 순서: Can < Could < Would (please). 수업에서는 보통 Could와 Would를 많이 씁니다. Can은 친한 사이에서만!

“Would you mind…?” + Gerund (-ing)

The Most Polite Request Form

Would you mind + verb-ing...? = “~해 주시면 안 될까요?”

Would you mind turning the music down?
Would you mind keeping your cat inside?

Would you mind not + verb-ing...? = 부정 요청

Would you mind not parking your car in my space?
주의: mind 뒤에는 반드시 동명사(-ing)가 옵니다. 원형동사 ✗
“Would you mind to turn...” → ✗ 틀림!
“Would you mind turning...” → ✓ 맞음!

Request Forms: Comparison

Modal + Base Form Would you mind + -ing
Can you keep your cat inside? Would you mind keeping your cat inside?
Could you turn the music down? Would you mind turning the music down?
Would you please park in your space? Would you mind parking in your space?
Could you not make noise? Would you mind not making noise?
응답 차이 주의:
• Can/Could/Would 요청 → “Sure.” / “OK.” / “No problem.”
• Would you mind...? → “No, not at all.” (아니요 = 괜찮아요, 해 드릴게요) — “Yes”라고 하면 거절이 됩니다!

Common Mistakes: Requests

✗ Would you mind to turn down the music?
✓ Would you mind turning down the music?
mind 뒤에는 to부정사가 아닌 동명사(-ing)를 사용합니다.
✗ "Would you mind closing the door?" — "Yes, sure!"
✓ "Would you mind closing the door?" — "No, not at all." / "Sure, no problem."
“Would you mind...?”에 “Yes”로 대답하면 “네, 싫어요(신경 쓰여요)”라는 뜻! 동의할 때는 “No, not at all”이 올바른 표현입니다.
✗ Can you mind not parking here?
✓ Would you mind not parking here?
“mind”는 Would와만 함께 씁니다. Can you mind... / Could you mind...는 틀린 표현입니다.
✗ Would you mind don’t smoke here?
✓ Would you mind not smoking here?
부정형은 “not + -ing”입니다. don’t를 쓰지 마세요.

Reading: Hotel Madness

Hotel Madness: The Crazy Things People Say!

There are about 500,000 hotels around the world. Every day, receptionists, servers, chefs, and managers work in these hotels looking after their guests. Guests often make special requests for things like an extra-large bed or a room with a view of the water. And sometimes people complain when something is not satisfactory.

In the U.S., around two-thirds of all complaints are about the noise that other guests are making. Sometimes, guests’ requests and complaints can make a hotel worker’s job almost impossible!

Here are some very weird requests that hotel workers have actually heard:

  • “Would you mind lending me your suit tomorrow?”
  • “Could one of the staff give my daughter a hand with her homework?”
  • “Can you please fill my bath with chocolate milk?”
  • “I’d like chicken for dinner, please, but only the right leg.”

Some hotel guests are also very good at finding (or imagining) problems! These are some of their crazy complaints:

  • At a London hotel, 40 miles from the coast: “I can’t see the ocean from my room.”
  • At a Portuguese hotel: “My bed is way too comfortable. I keep oversleeping and missing the best part of the day!”
  • To a receptionist in the middle of the night: “I haven’t been able to sleep at all! My wife won’t stop snoring!”

Reading Comprehension

Are these statements True or False? Click to check.

2. Most complaints in U.S. hotels are about noise.

Correct! Around two-thirds of complaints are about noise from other guests.

3. One hotel guest asked to borrow an employee’s dress.

The guest asked to borrow a suit, not a dress.

4. One person wasn’t happy because he kept getting up too early.

He was oversleeping (sleeping too long) because the bed was too comfortable.

5. Someone complained about not taking the right things to go sightseeing.

That’s not mentioned in the passage. Complaints were about ocean views, comfortable beds, and snoring.

Key Expressions: Apologizing

Four strategies for apologizing:

StrategyExample
Give an excuse
변명하기		 “I’m sorry. I didn’t realize…”
Admit a mistake
실수 인정		 “I forgot I left it there.”
Make an offer
해결 제안		 “I’ll take it out right now.”
Make a promise
약속하기		 “I promise I’ll make sure to…”
사과의 4단계: ① 변명 → ② 실수 인정 → ③ 즉각 해결 제안 → ④ 재발 방지 약속. 실제로 이 4가지를 조합하면 효과적인 사과가 됩니다.

Pronunciation: Stress in Two-Part Verbs

In two-part verbs, both words receive equal stress:

PICK · UP   your things.

TURN · OFF   the light.

CLEAN · UP   the kitchen.

PUT · AWAY   your clothes.

With pronouns, the pronoun is unstressed:

PICK · them · UP.

TURN · it · OFF.

강세 규칙: 구동사에서 동사와 전치사 모두 강하게 발음! 하지만 대명사(it, them)는 약하게 발음됩니다.

Today’s Takeaways

  • Two-part verbs: verb + particle (turn down, pick up, put away…)
  • Pronoun rule: pronoun goes between verb and particle → turn it down ✓
  • will for requests: “I’ll turn it down.” = willingness to help
  • Polite requests: Can < Could < Would (please) < Would you mind -ing...?
  • “Would you mind...?” answer: “No, not at all” = Yes, I’ll do it!
