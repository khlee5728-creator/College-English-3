Word Formation: Cooking Verbs

Many cooking verbs form related adjectives and nouns. Learn all forms!

bake
→
bake (verb) → baked (adj.) → baker (noun: person) I bake bread. / baked potatoes / The baker opens at 6 a.m.
fry
→
fry (verb) → fried (adj.) → stir-fry (noun/verb) Fry the onions. / fried chicken / vegetable stir-fry
grill
→
grill (verb/noun) → grilled (adj.) Grill the fish. / grilled steak / a charcoal grill

Grammar 1: Simple Past vs. Present Perfect

Simple Past

Definite time in the past

  • I tried sushi last month.
  • I ate at a Thai restaurant last night.
  • She cooked galbi yesterday.

Present Perfect

Up to now (no specific time)

  • Have you ever tried sushi?
  • I have never eaten frog legs.
  • She has cooked galbi many times.
핵심 차이:
• 단순과거: 과거 특정 시점의 경험 (yesterday, last week, in 2020)
• 현재완료: 지금까지의 경험 (ever, never, before, many times) — 언제인지 중요하지 않음

Simple Past vs. Present Perfect: Comparison

Present Perfect (경험) Simple Past (구체적 사실)
Have you ever eaten frog legs? Yes, I tried them last month.
Have you ever been to a Vietnamese restaurant? No, but I ate at a Thai restaurant last night.
Has she ever made pasta from scratch? Yes, she made it for my birthday.
I’ve never tried Indian food. I didn’t eat anything spicy when I was a kid.
패턴 정리: 먼저 Have you ever...?로 경험을 물어보고, 대답할 때 구체적인 과거 이야기는 단순과거로 전환합니다.

Present Perfect: Sentence Analysis

Structure: have/has + past participle

I have tried Korean food many times.
She has never eaten snails.
Have you ever been to a Vietnamese restaurant?

Compare with Simple Past

I tried them last month.
핵심: 현재완료 = have/has + 과거분사(p.p.)
• try → tried / tried • eat → ate / eaten • be → was / been • make → made / made

Grammar 2: Sequence Adverbs

Use these adverbs to describe the order of steps in a process (like a recipe).

First, the first step in a process
Then, the next step after that
Next, / After that, the following steps
Finally, the last step
순서 부사: 요리법, 절차 등을 설명할 때 단계를 나타내는 표현입니다.
• First (먼저) → Then (그 다음) → Next / After that (그 후) → Finally (마지막으로)

Common Errors: Past vs. Present Perfect

I have eaten sushi yesterday.
I ate sushi yesterday.
yesterday는 구체적 시간 → 단순과거를 사용합니다.
Did you ever eat frog legs?
Have you ever eaten frog legs?
ever(지금까지)는 현재완료와 함께 사용합니다.
I have went to that restaurant before.
I have been to that restaurant before.
현재완료에서는 과거분사를 씁니다: go → went(과거) / been(과거분사)
I have never try Indian food.
I have never tried Indian food.
have + 과거분사: try → tried (동사원형 X)

Common Errors: Sequence Adverbs

At first, boil the water.
First, boil the water.
“At first”는 “처음에는 (~했지만)”의 뜻으로 순서와 다릅니다. 레시피에서는 First를 씁니다.
First boil the water then add the pasta.
First, boil the water. Then, add the pasta.
순서 부사 뒤에는 쉼표(,)를 붙입니다.
Finally, boil the water. First, add the cheese.
First, boil the water. Finally, add the cheese.
First가 첫 단계, Finally가 마지막 단계입니다. 순서를 혼동하지 마세요.

Reading: Pizza — The World’s Favorite Food

Pizza: The World’s Favorite Food

Food, and the way we eat it, is always changing. As society develops, we learn new ways of growing, processing, and cooking food. Food must fit modern lifestyles and local tastes, too.

The pizza we recognize today first appeared in Italy in 1889. A famous baker from Naples made a special pizza for the Italian royal family. Queen Margherita loved the dish so much that the baker named it after her. Since then, this simple meal of bread, cheese, and tomato has traveled the world.

Pizza began its journey to New York in the 1890s, when many Italians moved to New York. The first pizzeria opened in 1905. At first it was only popular with Italians, but by the late 1940s, Americans discovered a taste for it. Today, they spend an incredible $37 billion a year on pizza.

Pizza continued its travels around the world, adapting all the time. In Sweden, it is not unusual to have peanuts and bananas on your pizza. Japan is a nation of seafood lovers, so they love octopus and squid. In the Philippines, they like mango on their pizza.

Expressions: Talking About Food

Asking About Experiences

  • Have you ever tried…? — Yes, I have. / No, I haven’t.
  • Have you ever eaten…? — Yes, I tried it last week.
  • Have you ever been to…? — No, I’ve never been there.
  • How did you like it? — It was delicious! / I didn’t like it.

Ordering in a Restaurant

  • Have you decided on an appetizer yet?
  • I’ll have… / I think I’ll have the…
  • A small order of…, please.
  • And you, sir/ma’am?

Expressions: Describing Recipes

How to Describe a Recipe

  • To make…, you need… (재료 소개)
  • First, you… / Then, you… (순서 설명)
  • It takes about… minutes. (시간 안내)
  • It’s easy/difficult to make. (난이도)
Example: “To make guacamole, you need tomatoes, avocado, and lime. First, chop the tomatoes and onion. Then, scoop out the avocado. Next, squeeze the lime and mix everything. Finally, season with salt.”
