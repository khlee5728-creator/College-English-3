Expressions: Giving Movie Opinions

Sharing Your Opinion

  • I'm interested in... — ...에 관심이 있어요
  • I'm not interested in... — ...에 관심이 없어요
  • I thought it was... — ...라고 생각했어요
  • I'm fascinated by... — ...에 매료되어 있어요
  • I was bored by... — ...가 지루했어요

Describing Movies with Relative Clauses

  • It's the movie which/that... — 그것은 ...한 영화예요
  • It's about a man/woman who/that... — ...하는 남자/여자에 관한 거예요
A: Have you seen any good movies lately?
B: Yes! I saw a thriller that was absolutely terrifying.
A: What's it about?
B: It's about a woman who discovers a secret about her neighbor.

Grammar: Participles as Adjectives (-ed vs -ing)

-ed adjectives describe how you feel.
-ing adjectives describe the thing that causes the feeling.
-ed = how you feel  |  -ing = what causes the feeling
-ing (describes the thing) -ed (describes the feeling) Example
boring bored The movie is boring. → I am bored.
exciting excited The game is exciting. → I am excited.
fascinating fascinated The story is fascinating. → I am fascinated.
terrifying terrified The scene is terrifying. → I am terrified.
amusing amused The joke is amusing. → I am amused.
disgusting disgusted The food is disgusting. → I am disgusted.
surprising surprised The ending is surprising. → I am surprised.
-ed는 '~한 감정을 느끼는', -ing는 '~한 감정을 주는'의 의미입니다. 예: boring(지루하게 만드는) vs bored(지루함을 느끼는)

Grammar: Relative Clauses (who/which/that)

Use who or that for people.
Use which or that for things.
Relative clauses give extra information about a noun.
noun (person) + who/that + verb  |  noun (thing) + which/that + verb
  • She's the actress who won the award. (person)
  • It's a movie that everyone is talking about. (thing)
  • The film which we saw last night was amazing. (thing)
  • He's the director who/that made my favorite movie. (person)
  • It's about a man who travels through time. (person)
  • It's the kind of movie that makes you think. (thing)
관계대명사: 사람에게 who/that, 사물에게 which/that을 사용합니다. 관계대명사는 명사에 대한 추가 정보를 제공합니다.

Expressions: Speculation & Rules

Hypothesizing / Speculating

  • It might mean... — ...를 의미할 수도 있어요 (possible)
  • It may mean... — ...를 의미할 수 있어요 (possible)
  • It could mean... — ...를 의미할 수 있어요 (possible)
  • That must mean... — ...를 의미하는 게 틀림없어요 (certain)
  • That probably means... — 아마 ...를 의미할 거예요 (likely)
  • Maybe it means... — 어쩌면 ...를 의미할 수도 있어요 (uncertain)

Permission, Prohibition & Obligation

  • You can... / You're allowed to... — ...해도 됩니다 (permission)
  • You can't... / You aren't allowed to... — ...하면 안 됩니다 (prohibition)
  • You have to... / You've got to... — ...해야 합니다 (obligation)
추측 표현: might/may/could (가능성), must (확실한 추측). 규칙 표현: can/allowed to (허가), can't/not allowed to (금지), have to/got to (의무).

Grammar: Modals for Speculation

We use modal verbs to speculate (guess) about the meaning of body language and situations.
Modal Meaning Certainty Example
must I'm almost sure Very high (90%+) He's yawning. He must be tired.
may It's possible Medium (50%) She's quiet. She may be thinking.
might It's possible Medium-low (40%) He's frowning. He might be confused.
could It's possible Medium-low (40%) She's smiling. It could mean she's happy.
Subject + must / may / might / could + base verb
  • He's scratching his head. That must mean he's confused.
  • She's tapping her foot. She might be nervous.
  • He's biting his nails. He could be anxious.
  • They're clapping. They must be happy with the performance.
must는 확실한 추측 (~임에 틀림없다), might/may/could는 가능성 있는 추측 (~일 수도 있다)입니다.

Grammar: Permission, Prohibition & Obligation

We use different modal expressions to talk about what is allowed, what is forbidden, and what is required.
Category Structure Example
Permission can / be allowed to You can use your phone during the break.
Permission be allowed to You are allowed to park here after 6 PM.
Prohibition can't / not allowed to You can't smoke inside the building.
Prohibition not allowed to You aren't allowed to take photos in the museum.
Obligation have to / have got to You have to wear a seat belt in the car.
Obligation have got to You've got to follow the speed limit.
허가: can / be allowed to (~해도 된다). 금지: can't / not allowed to (~하면 안 된다). 의무: have to / have got to (~해야 한다).

Activity: Movie Scene Charades

Act It Out!

Work in groups of 4-5. Take turns acting out a movie scene without speaking. The other group members must guess:

  • What genre is it? "It must be a horror movie!" / "It could be an action movie."
  • What is happening? "The character might be running from something."
  • How does the character feel? "He must be terrified!" / "She could be excited."

Useful Language

  • It must be... because he/she looks [emotion].
  • It might be... a movie that/which...
  • It could be about a person who...
  • The character looks terrified / excited / confused / exhausted.
그룹에서 한 명이 영화 장면을 말 없이 연기하면, 나머지가 추측 표현을 사용해서 장르, 내용, 감정을 맞혀 보세요.

Activity: Rules Around the World

Body Language & Rules in Different Countries

Discuss with your group. Think about differences in body language and rules between countries.

  • Gestures: In some countries, a thumbs up is positive. In others, it might be rude. Do you know any examples?
  • Eye contact: In Korea, avoiding eye contact with elders is polite. In Western countries, eye contact must be maintained.
  • Personal space: In some cultures, you have to stand far apart. In others, standing close is normal.
  • Dining rules: What are you allowed to / not allowed to do at the table in different countries?

Useful Structures

  • In Korea, you have to use both hands when giving something to an elder.
  • In some countries, you aren't allowed to eat with your left hand.
  • A nod might mean "yes" in one country but "no" in another.
  • Pointing with your finger could be considered rude in some cultures.
나라마다 다른 바디랭귀지와 규칙에 대해 토론해 보세요. 추측 표현과 허가/금지/의무 표현을 모두 사용하세요.
